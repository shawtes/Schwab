About the book
**Alpha Trader** by Brent Donnelly is an essential guide for
navigating the tumultuous world of trading—a complex and
ever-evolving realm where intellect and technology converge.
Recognizing that trading is not just a contest of knowledge but
also a test of mindset, methodology, and math, this book offers
actionable insights and real-life anecdotes to elevate your
trading skills. With a focus on critical topics such as strategy
versus tactics, market sentiment, and risk management,
Donnelly equips traders—whether novices or veterans—with
the tools to foster self-awareness, discipline, and adaptability.
Drawing from over two decades of professional trading
experience, Donnelly's engaging writing style makes each
concept accessible and compelling. **Alpha Trader** is your
roadmap to not just surviving but thriving in the high-stakes
arena of trading. Unleash your potential and discover what it
truly means to be an Alpha Trader.
About the author
Brent Donnelly is a seasoned currency trader with a career
spanning since 1995, currently serving as a senior FX trader at
HSBC in New York. He authors the respected macro and FX
daily newsletter, AM/FX, and has held key positions in major
financial institutions, including head of G10 Spot Trading at
Citi and global head of G10 FX Trading at Nomura. Brent
possesses extensive expertise across various financial
instruments, including spot FX, interest rate products, FX
options, equities, and commodities. Renowned for his insights,
his analysis has been featured in prestigious publications such
as The Economist, The Wall Street Journal, Financial Times,
Bloomberg, and CNBC. In addition to his trading
accomplishments, Brent has also made his mark in creative
media as the creator of the animated series “Daft Planet,”
which aired in Canada.

Chapter 1 Summary : A quick story
A Quick Story Before We Start
Setting the Scene
On May 6, 2010, a trader in Fairfield County, Connecticut,
starts his day early amidst the cold, damp air. Despite a tough
year grappling with a shaky Eurozone, he prepares for a
significant trading day with high expectations fueled by his
insights on USDJPY.
Trader’s Strategy and Market Conditions
The trader, frustrated with the Euro, shifts focus to USDJPY,
anticipating its decline amidst global risk aversion. He has a
short position but is close to being stopped out, feeling the
pressure of market volatility.
A Sudden Market Shift
During a morning run, the trader receives alarming news that
USDJPY has suddenly dropped significantly. Upon returning
to his desk, he realizes he made a substantial profit of $2.8
million before impulsively deciding to go long on the same
currency pair.
The Flash Crash
Unexpectedly, the stock market plummets, triggering a
drastic fall in USDJPY. The trader’s P&L swings
dramatically, leading to frustration and anger as he loses his
profit in a matter of minutes.
Regaining Focus
In a moment of clarity amidst the chaos, the trader recognizes
the need to refocus. He returns to his initial strategy, buying
USDJPY at a calculated price after assessing the market,
which eventually rebounds.
Lessons from the Day
The day's tumultuous trading reflects the thin line between
success and failure in trading. The story encapsulates the core
messages of self-awareness and discipline necessary for
traders, reinforcing the aim of "Alpha Trader" to help traders
of all levels understand both markets and themselves.
Conclusion and Author's Note
The author hopes to share ongoing insights and updates for
readers interested in enhancing their trading journey. The
book is crafted to aid traders in maximizing their potential
through understanding market dynamics and personal biases,
promising to be entertaining and educational.

CHAPTER 2: IF IT WAS EASY, IT WOULDN’T
PAY SO WELL
Achieving success in trading is challenging. It’s comparable
to becoming a professional athlete or a successful author,
with many aspiring but few succeeding. The low success
rates among independent and professional traders highlight
the steep barriers to consistent profitability, primarily due to
trading being a negative-sum game impacted by high
transaction costs and risks.
Success rates for day traders are estimated between 3% and
5%, with institutional traders faring better due to training and
a competitive edge. Studies indicate that trading success is
rare and requires a combination of innate skill, hard work,
and perseverance.
WHAT PERCENTAGE OF TRADERS SUCCEED?
The author cites various studies, affirming that success in

traders fail to generate positive returns, while even
institutional traders operate under challenging conditions.
The text also parallels trading challenges with other
high-stakes fields like sports and writing, where the success
rates are comparably low.

UNDERSTAND MICROSTRUCTURE
Donnelly stresses that successful traders have a robust
understanding of microstructure, which encompasses market
participants, liquidity, intraday activity patterns, and
volatility. He details the types of market participants (retail,
institutional, hedge funds, corporates, and central banks) and
the significance of liquidity, including transaction costs and
gap risks. The chapter elaborates on the relationship between
market volatility and the events that impact it. Traders are
encouraged to observe their specific market's patterns and
adapt their strategies accordingly.
UNDERSTAND NARRATIVE
This chapter focuses on understanding narratives in trading,
describing how market stories influence pricing. Donnelly
explains the importance of identifying the dominant narrative
and knowing how it can shift over time. He introduces the
narrative cycle, marking various stages; from under the radar
to peak and decline. Key strategies include reading
extensively to gauge sentiment and building a network of
knowledgeable contacts to continually refine one's
understanding of narratives.
UNDERSTAND TECHNICALS, SENTIMENT
AND POSITIONING
Donnelly outlines the tactical aspect of trading, detailing how
technical analysis can be used for execution and risk
management rather than trade selection. He provides insights
into significant reference points like NewsPivots and support
and resistance levels. Sentiment indicators are important
during inflection points, but traders should not base decisions
solely on sentiment without considering other analyses. He
advocates for a comprehensive approach that integrates
fundamentals, narratives, sentiment, and technicals.
YOU FEELING LUCKY, PUNK?
In this chapter, Donnelly delves into risk management as the
most critical component of trading success. He emphasizes
the necessity of collecting and analyzing P&L data to
understand risks and to implement effective risk management
systems. He outlines the importance of managing risk across
various timeframes, from yearly to daily stop losses, and
stresses the need for adapting position sizing based on
various factors, including conviction and market conditions.
BRINGING IT ALL TOGETHER
Donnelly wraps up by detailing the complete lifecycle of a
trade, from idea generation to execution and performance
analysis. He emphasizes the importance of preparation,
filtering ideas through multiple lenses, and ensuring that
tactics are in place to maximize execution. The culmination
of a robust process lies in the ability to adapt to changing
market conditions while maintaining a clear plan and
assessing risks effectively.
Overall, the methodology and mathematics presented in this

Critical Thinking
Key Point:The integration of multiple analytical

Critical Interpretation:Donnelly underscores the vital
role of incorporating various perspectives—technical
analysis, sentiment, narratives, and fundamentals—in
trading strategies. This multifaceted approach can
enhance decision-making and adaptiveness, yet readers
should be critical of the assertion that this is the best

CHAPTER 14: ADAPT OR DIE
Adapting to market changes is vital for long-term success;
rigid methodologies can lead to failure. Traders must learn to
recognize shifts in market structure and develop new
strategies accordingly. The importance of adaptation is
highlighted through historical examples, such as the changes
in trend-following strategies and the emergence of algo
trading.
ORDER VS. CHAOS
Markets oscillate between order and chaos, with chaotic
times presenting temporary inefficiencies and high-risk
opportunities. Key strategies for trading in fast markets
include correct position sizing, maintaining flexibility, and
recognizing that typical overbought/oversold signals may not
apply during crises.
-
Trading Fast Markets
: Requires careful position sizing. Understand current
volatility to adjust stops and positions effectively.
-
Open Mind
: Be prepared for extreme market movements—don't
underestimate potential volatility.
-
No Overbought/Oversold in Crisis
: Traditional signals are unreliable; recognize the difference
between normal and crisis-level risk aversion.
VOLATILITY AND RISK MANAGEMENT
Adapting position size based on volatility is crucial. More
volatile environments require smaller positions, while less
volatile markets may accommodate larger positions. Using
tools like spreadsheets can help traders adjust their strategies
effectively.
RANGEBound VS. TRENDING MARKETS
Understanding whether a market is trending or range-bound
can inform trading strategies. Utilize simple technical
analysis to identify market conditions and make necessary
adjustments.
ADAPTATION TO MACRO CHANGES
Traders must remain vigilant to changes in market
methodology as existing strategies may lose efficacy over
time. The necessity of adaptation is underscored by
examining substantial historical shifts in market structure.

Chapter 7 Summary : One last story
ONE LAST STORY BEFORE YOU GO
Context and Setting
In 2000, during a period of American optimism, the author
reflects on his experiences as a 27-year-old day trader in
Toronto, Canada. This period predates various financial
crises and represents a time when quantitative easing was
still a novel concept.
The Trading Day
On July 26, 2000, the author is at a day trading office and is
preparing for the closing bell at 4 p.m. After a significant
drop in the NASDAQ (from over 5,000 to 3,200), the market
is on a recovery path due to Fed rate cuts and excitement
around tech stocks, particularly JDS Uniphase (JDSU).
Having transformed a $25,000 investment into a portfolio of
$350,000, the author holds an 800-share position in JDSU.
Anticipation and Strategy
As JDSU is set to be added to the S&P 500, the author
describes the expected demand surge as index funds adjust
their holdings. With adrenaline high, he sells his shares just
before the close to avoid potential price crashes that often
follow index additions.
Arbitrage Opportunity
In the final moments of trading, the author discovers a
pricing inefficiency: JDSU is listed at different prices on two
exchange platforms. Acting quickly, he executes a series of
trades, profiting a total of $48,500 in a frenzy of arbitrage
before the market closes.
Aftermath and Reflection on Market Changes
The author reflects on the unprecedented nature of the trade,
noting that JDSU never regains its peak price, ultimately
losing 99% of its value. Despite this success, the author soon
faces the harsh reality of market changes, including a rapid
decline in NASDAQ stocks and the introduction of
decimalization, prompting the eventual shutdown of his
trading account.
Lessons Learned
Four key lessons emerge from this experience:
1. Market inefficiencies do not last forever; take advantage
while you can.
2. Overconfidence can lead to significant losses as the market
corrects.
3. Trading can be thrilling but requires caution.
4. Indexes do not provide the complete picture of market
dynamics.
Chapter 8 Summary : Coda
CODA
The text reflects on the insights gained from a trader's
experience during the 2010 Flash Crash, detailing how
decisions, biases, and market dynamics can shape a trading
day's outcome.
Trader's Context
On May 6, 2010, a trader in Connecticut navigates a
challenging environment marked by the Eurozone Crisis and
a bearish outlook on stocks. He focuses on shorting USDJPY,
expecting it to decline amid rising risk aversion and
misleading market behavior.
Trading Strategies and Biases
-
Anchoring Bias
: The trader grapples with his prior convictions about the
EURUSD trade's viability despite substantial losses.
-
Relative Value Trading
: He relies on US bond yields as an indicator for USDJPY,
observing unusual divergence that signifies a strong short
opportunity.
-
Round Number Bias
: Noticing USDJPY's resistance around 94.99 reinforces his
decision-making.
Market Turbulence
After a slow morning, the trader unexpectedly sees USDJPY
plummet by 100 points while out for a run. Upon returning,
he successfully locks in a +$2.8 million profit but lets
impulsive thoughts lead him to initiate a long position,
undermining his earlier disciplined approach.
Emotional Rollercoaster
As the S&P 500 drops sharply, USDJPY follows suit. The
trader's position rapidly deteriorates, illustrating the dangers
of emotional trading. His hard-earned profit vanishes within
minutes, and he feels the emotional turmoil of regret for
deviating from his plan.
Finding Clarity
Realizing the need for rationality amidst chaos, he steps back
to regain focus. The panic subsides as markets stabilize,
prompting him to reassess and take a calculated long position
in USDJPY, indicating a return to disciplined trading.
Outcome and Reflection
The trader successfully reverses his position, recovering
losses and restoring his earlier profits. The narrative
encapsulates the dichotomy of trading—where sound
judgment can lead to success, but impulsive decisions may
result in losses. Ultimately, the day serves as a microcosm of
trading psychology, emphasizing the importance of
discipline, strategy, and self-awareness.
Conclusion
The chapter concludes with a poignant reminder of the
complexities in trading, where outcomes are often influenced
by both good and bad decisions, luck, and market dynamics,
encapsulating many pivotal lessons for traders.

21 Ways to Succeed at Trading
1. Adapt to changing markets.
2. Maintain rational thought processes.
3. Cultivate self-awareness.
4. Avoid catastrophic losses.
5. Study metacognition to understand thinking patterns.
6. Accept being wrong as part of learning.
7. Clearly articulate trading edge.
8. Prioritize passion for trading over monetary gain.
9. Differentiate between process and outcome.
10. Implement a tight/aggressive trading strategy.
11. Gain expertise in specific markets.
12. Foster creativity and independent thought.
13. Grasp variance and the metagame dynamics.
14. Persevere through setbacks.

16. Develop and adhere to a disciplined plan.
17. Identify and rectify biased thinking.
18. Apply a systematic risk management approach.
19. Maintain self-belief without succumbing to

20. Solve problems with above-average quantitative skills.
21. Possess the courage to make significant trades.
13 Ways to Fail at Trading
1. Engage in overtrading.
2. Lack a distinguishable trading edge.
3. Fear being wrong.
4. Exhibit overconfidence.
5. Hesitate to execute trades.
6. Depend excessively on basic indicators.
7. Prioritize trade ideas over risk management.
8. Use uniform position sizes for all trades.
9. Act impulsively and without discipline.
10. Rely on fast, instinctive thinking (System 1).
11. Neglect reading and continuous learning.
12. Deceive themselves about their abilities.
13. Approach trading as gambling.
Conclusion
Approach each trading day with a positive mindset, focus on
the work, act rationally, and repeat the process for ongoing
success.
